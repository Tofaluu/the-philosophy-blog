The Task of the Philosopher

A radical departure from the traditional imagery of the philosopher as merely a critic or sceptic, Friedrich Nietzsche suggests the philosopher as the creator of values. According to Nietzsche, the philosopher's work is not to believe the current truths but to give law to new values to direct humanity's future. This essay critically analyses this claim, using one argument to support and one to oppose Nietzsche's proposition.

Argument For: The Philosopher as a Creator of Values

Nietzsche's interpretation of the philosopher's role defines the philosopher as a creator of values, thus situating philosophy as an active and transforming force. For Nietzsche, true philosophers are "commanders and legislators” not bound by the dogma of inherited truths but tasked with creating new paradigms of understanding (Nietzsche, Beyond Good and Evil 106). In this view, philosophy is not, as there has long been a tendency to believe, a purely derivative or reflective discipline. Philosophers are imagined as great builders of cultural and existential meaning, great wielders of what Nietzsche meant by the will to power. With the idea of the philosopher's creative agency, Nietzsche insists on innovation as a remedy against evolving societal and existential challenges. In such a context, Nietzsche's model makes the philosopher a necessary progress and reform agent. It is also a direct connection of philosophy to life and, thus, a practice of vitality and dynamism rather than cogitation.

What makes this argument strong is an acknowledgment of the transformative power of philosophy. Placing philosophers as creators who bridge thought with action and keeps philosophy related to humanity as long as the philosophers continue to create. It is also opposed to the reductive pragmatism of positivism, which typically demotes philosophy (logically) lower than the empirical sciences. Instead, Nietzsche drills philosophy back into the significance of living a life with meaning.

Argument Against: The Risk of Authoritarianism

Although Nietzsche’s vision is inspirational, his characteristically grandiose approach to an elite intelligence of great poets and artists who would make civilization anew is as vulnerable to the criticism that it would lead to authoritarianism as is the familiar socialist commonwealth of noble minds. Nietzsche endangers the philosopher's burden of authority by elevating him to the position of legislative power. This leads to an issue about subjectivity and fallibility within human nature. Nietzsche himself acknowledges that philosophical doctrines often stem from personal prejudices, whims, or "fervent wish[es]" masked as truths (Nietzsche, Beyond Good and Evil 8). The problem arises when philosophers are granted the power to impose arbitrary or harmfully rigid systems in the name of innovation.

Furthermore, this approach cuts against philosophy's collaborative and communal nature. Wilhelm Dilthey’s critique offers a counterpoint, emphasizing that philosophy gains legitimacy through its capacity to unify diverse worldviews and to ground itself in a "universally valid worldview" (Dilthey, The Essence of Philosophy 239). While Nietzsche’s model might leave us isolated from one another, and only connected through self as the fundamental reality, Dilthey finds philosophy within the continued human communal enterprise of leading meaningful lives with a communal dream to make things better. By taking this collaborative approach to exploring philosophical insights and sharing the validation for their existence, this framework reduces the risk of ideological extremism replacing philosophical insights.

Conclusion

An inspiring vision of what philosophy might be able to do, Nietzsche presents his definition of the philosopher's task — creator of values. This leads to the transformative nature of philosophy and the fact that it can help us break into uncharted territories. The model, though, is not without risk. Nietzsche’s framework, wherein individual will is over communal dialogue, can prove to be used to abuse power. It is possible that both tasks of the philosopher are necessary, and priority shifts depending on the conditions of the philosophers. Being patrons of communal ideals when conditions allow and a creator of values when a rapid transformation is necessary.
